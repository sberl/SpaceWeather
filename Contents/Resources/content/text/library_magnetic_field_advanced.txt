<div id="research">
		
	<div id="term">
		<h2>Magnetic Field</h2>
	</div>
	
	<div id="term-content">
	
		<div id="left-pane">
		
			<span id="definition">
				<p>
					<strong>Definition:</strong><br />
					A region in which magnetic forces can be observed. Magnetic Poles: Either of two regions in a magnet at which the magnet's 
					field is most intense. The two regions have opposing polarities, which we label "north" and "south", after the two poles on the Earth. 
				</p>
			</span>
			
			<span id="more-information">
				<p>
					<strong>More Information:</strong><br />
					A magnetic field is the 3-dimensional region around a magnet within which ferrous (magnetically sensitive) metals, electrical currents, 
					<strong><a href="event:loadToolCItem('Research','ResearchPlasmahtm')">plasma</a></strong>, or other magnets are either attracted or repelled.
				</p>
				<img src="../images/library_magnetic_field_02.gif" alt="Magnetic Field Animation" />
				<small>An animation of the magnetic forces surrounding a magnet.</small>
				<br />
				<img class="noborder" src="../images/library_magnetic_field_04.jpg" alt="Magnetic Field" />
				<small>Two magnets with North and South poles labeled.</small>
				<br /><br />
				<div class="right medium caption">
					<img src="../images/library_magnetic_field_03.jpg" alt="Magnetosphere" />
					<small>A drawing of Earth's magnetosphere.</small>
				</div>
				<p>
					The field consists of layers of magnetic flow that originate from the magnet's north pole and move in arcs to its south pole.
				</p>
				<p>
					The Earth's core produces a magnetic field known as the <strong>
					<a href="event:loadToolCItem('Research','ResearchMagnetospherehtm')">magnetosphere</a></strong>.
				</p>
				<p>
					During a mission to the Moon (or to other locations in the solar system) a spacecraft is outside the protection of Earth's magnetosphere.
				</p>
			</span>
			
		</div>
